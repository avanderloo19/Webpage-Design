Gerenuk- Litocranius walleri

THE LOOKS OF THE GERENUK

The long neck and long, thin legs of gerenuks are the features they are most known for and therefore make them one of the world's most recognizable antelopes. The fur is a pale brown with white along the stomach and inner legs. There are smaller, darker spots of fur on the knees of the forelegs and at the end of their tail. The head is small with larger ears. On the head there is a dark spot around the eyes Only males of this species have small horns. Both genders are similar in size but the males have more muscules than females causing them to weigh more than their female counterparts.

GERENUK DESCRIPTION

A Gerenuk is a type of antelope that belongs to the bovid family. There are two subspecies of gerenuk that can be found in Africa, usually in the East. Gerenuks inhabit semi-arid areas, deserts and dry bushy savannas. The number of gerenuks in the wild is decreasing due to the uncontrolled hunting and habitat destruction by humans. Gerenuk is classified as near threatened, which means that it can become endangered in the near future.

CONSERVATION

Habitat loss and fragmentation caused by humans are currently the main dangers to the gerenuk populations. The gerenuk is presently listed as Near Threatened for conservation status, and an estimated 95,000 remain among four countries. Purposeful wildlife conservation has kept gerenuks from becoming an endangered species.

As human populations grow, they expand settlements, roads, and agriculture, resulting in lost or segmented habitats for gerenuks and other wildlife. This makes it more difficult for them to find food, shelter themselves from predators, and locate any mates For more information please visit The Saint Louis Zoo

SOLUTIONS FROM AMERICAN WILDLIFE FOUNDATION (AWF) . . .

  • Work with governments.
    • African Wildlife Foundation engages with government entities to help plan and propose alternative solutions to habitat fragmentation. In the case of the Serengeti Highway, we provide our scientists and researchers as resources to assist in proper planning to ensure a balance between modernization and conservation.

  • Expand conservation tourism.
    • AWF brings together private investors and communities to construct tourism lodges like Satao Elerai, a luxury lodge situated on 5,000 acres in Kenya. The land is protected, so gerenuks and other wildlife can roam safely and freely, and the revenue is reinvested into the community and into local wildlife conservation.
